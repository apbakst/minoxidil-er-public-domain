Executive Summary

This document compiles comprehensive counter-arguments to every major defense Veradermics could raise for their extended-release minoxidil patent. Each counter-argument is supported by Federal Circuit case law and prior art evidence.

1. Counter: "Modified Release Excludes IR - Claims Not Anticipated"

STRONG COUNTER

Veradermics argues: "The claims require 'modified release pharmaceutical formulation' - IR minoxidil doesn't qualify, so prior art IR studies (Fleishaker 1989) don't anticipate our claims."

Our Response: Five Independent Counter-Arguments

A. Inherency Doctrine Defeats This Defense

IR minoxidil inherently achieves the claimed PK parameters:

  • IR 2.5mg Cmax: 16.8 ng/mL (within claimed 0.25-20 ng/mL)
  • IR Tmax: 60 minutes (within claimed 30-360 minutes)

Under Schering Corp. v. Geneva Pharmaceuticals, 339 F.3d 1373 (Fed. Cir. 2003): "Anticipation cannot be avoided merely because an element is undisclosed and unrecognized in the reference, but is a deliberate or necessary consequence of the reference's disclosure."

B. The TRUE Claim Limitations Are the PK Parameters

The claims define the invention by functional outcomes (Cmax, Tmax ranges), not structural requirements. The term "modified release" is merely a label - the operative limitations are the PK parameters that IR minoxidil already achieves.

C. Any Oral Tablet "Modifies" Release vs. IV

Compared to intravenous administration (Tmax = 0), ANY oral formulation "modifies" release. Standard IR tablets with binders, fillers, and disintegrants technically modify how drug is released.

D. Specification Doesn't Limit "Modified Release" Narrowly

The specification lists multiple technologies without limitation (osmotic pumps, microencapsulation, enteric coatings, matrix systems). Under BRI, "modified release" encompasses any formulation modifying release.

E. Even If Limited to ER, Claims Are OBVIOUS

This is the "Claim Scope Dilemma" - if Veradermics argues narrow construction (ER only), the claims become obvious under Ground 2 of our IPR.

Key Cases:
  • King Pharmaceuticals v. Eon Labs, 616 F.3d 1267 (Fed. Cir. 2010) - Inherent anticipation of method claims
  • Atlas Powder Co. v. IRECO, 190 F.3d 1342 (Fed. Cir. 1999) - Discovery of unappreciated property doesn't create novelty
  • Phillips v. AWH Corp., 415 F.3d 1303 (Fed. Cir. 2005) - Claims construed by actual requirements, not labels

2. Counter: "No Reasonable Expectation of Success"

STRONG COUNTER

Veradermics argues: "A POSA had no reasonable expectation of success that ER minoxidil would achieve a therapeutic window between the hair growth threshold (1.62 ng/mL) and cardiac threshold (20 ng/mL)."

Our Response: Clinical Evidence PROVED Success Before Priority Date

A. 1,404+ Patients Already Successfully Treated

Study Date Patients Finding
Vano-Galvan (JAAD) 2021 1,404 98.3% continued treatment; no serious AEs
Jimenez-Cauhe 2020 442 70-100% response rates
Sinclair 2018 100 "Safe and effective" at 0.25mg

This is not "hope" - this is documented clinical success.

B. The Therapeutic Window Was Already Being Exploited

Dermatologists worldwide were prescribing low-dose oral minoxidil off-label. The window between efficacy and toxicity was functionally known even if not precisely quantified.

C. ER Formulation Outcomes Are Predictable

  • ER reduces Cmax - this is the definition of ER technology
  • The magnitude of reduction is calculable from release rate
  • HPMC matrix behavior is well-characterized with mathematical models

D. Minoxidil's Properties Made Success Predictable

  • High oral bioavailability (~90%)
  • Moderate half-life (4.2 hours) - ideal for ER
  • Linear pharmacokinetics
  • Well-characterized dose-response

E. Distinguish OSI Pharmaceuticals v. Apotex

OSI involved cancer treatment with >99.5% Phase II failure rate and no efficacy data. Minoxidil ER involves predictable formulation science with proven clinical efficacy - completely different.

Key Cases:
  • In re O'Farrell, 853 F.2d 894 (Fed. Cir. 1988) - "Obviousness does not require absolute predictability of success"
  • KSR v. Teleflex, 550 U.S. 398 (2007) - Reasonable expectation standard
  • Pfizer v. Apotex, 480 F.3d 1348 (Fed. Cir. 2007) - Finite options = reasonable expectation

3. Counter: "Prior Art Taught Away from Oral Minoxidil"

STRONG COUNTER

Veradermics argues: "The Loniten FDA label contains a BLACK BOX WARNING about cardiac effects, teaching away from oral minoxidil for cosmetic indications like hair loss."

Our Response: The Warning Addresses a Different Indication at Different Doses

A. Teaching Away Requires "Criticize, Discredit, or Discourage"

Under In re Fulton, 391 F.3d 1195 (Fed. Cir. 2004): "Mere disclosure of more than one alternative does not constitute teaching away."

The Loniten warning says NOTHING about:

  • Hair loss treatment
  • Low-dose use (0.25-5mg)
  • Cosmetic indications

B. Prior Art ACTIVELY ENCOURAGED Oral Minoxidil for Hair Loss

Publication Conclusion
Sinclair 2018 "Safe and effective in the treatment of FPHL"
Randolph & Tosti 2021 "Effective and well-tolerated treatment alternative"
Vano-Galvan 2021 "Good safety profile" - designated "JAAD Game Changer"

C. Widespread Clinical Adoption Proves No Teaching Away

  • >25% of dermatologists in Spain regularly prescribe it
  • Major academic centers (Cleveland Clinic, Mayo) adopted it
  • International consensus guidelines developed by 43 experts

If the art "taught away," why were thousands of doctors doing it?

D. Teaching Away from IR ≠ Teaching Away from ER

The safety concerns are about HIGH Cmax from IR. Making ER to reduce Cmax is the obvious solution to the known problem - not something taught against.

Key Cases:
  • In re Gurley, 27 F.3d 551 (Fed. Cir. 1994) - Teaching away requires discouragement from claimed path
  • Galderma v. Tolmar (Fed. Cir. 2013) - Preference for alternatives ≠ teaching away
  • Wyers v. Master Lock, 616 F.3d 1231 (Fed. Cir. 2010) - Must show actual discouragement

4. Counter: "Strong Secondary Considerations"

STRONG COUNTER

Veradermics argues: "Long-felt need for safe oral hair loss treatment, commercial success ($150M Series C, $256M IPO), and failure of others to develop ER minoxidil demonstrate non-obviousness."

Our Response: Each Secondary Consideration Fails

A. No Long-Felt Need - The Need Was Already Being Met

1,404+ patients were successfully treated with off-label oral minoxidil before October 2022. The "unmet need" was already being addressed by dermatologists worldwide.

B. Commercial Success Has No Nexus to Claimed Features

Under Fox Factory v. SRAM (Fed. Cir. 2019), success must be tied to claimed features, not external factors.

Veradermics' value derives from:

  • FDA approval status (regulatory, not technical)
  • Being "first FDA-approved" (marketing advantage)
  • Minoxidil's known efficacy (the drug, not the matrix)
  • Insurance coverage for Rx products

None of these relate to HPMC matrix formulation.

C. No "Failure of Others" - Others Weren't Trying

Under Stratoflex v. Aeroquip, must show others tried and failed.

No evidence anyone attempted and failed to make ER minoxidil. Lack of commercial development reflects business decisions (off-label market existed, generic minoxidil is $4/month), not technical barriers.

D. Weak Secondaries Cannot Overcome Strong Prima Facie Obviousness

Under Wyers v. Master Lock: "Secondary considerations cannot overcome a strong prima facie case of obviousness."

Key Cases:
  • Adapt Pharma v. Teva (Fed. Cir. 2022) - Long-felt need of 3 years insufficient
  • Ormco v. Align, 463 F.3d 1299 (Fed. Cir. 2006) - Success from unclaimed features irrelevant
  • Galderma v. Tolmar (Fed. Cir. 2013) - Blocking patents/exclusivity defeat nexus

5. Counter: "Doctrine of Equivalents" (Lipid Matrix Infringes)

STRONG COUNTER

Veradermics argues: "Even if lipid matrix doesn't literally infringe, it infringes under doctrine of equivalents because it performs the same function, in the same way, to achieve the same result."

Our Response: Seven Independent Defenses

A. Different "WAY" - Mechanisms Are Fundamentally Different

Property HPMC (Veradermics) Lipid Matrix (Ours)
Water interaction Hydrophilic - absorbs water Hydrophobic - repels water
Swelling Swells 2-4x in volume Does NOT swell
Gel formation Forms viscous hydrogel NO gel - remains solid
Release mechanism Diffusion through gel layer Diffusion through pores/channels

B. Prosecution History Estoppel

Any narrowing amendments or arguments during prosecution that emphasized HPMC properties create estoppel under Festo.

C. Disclosure-Dedication Rule

If the specification contemplated alternative mechanisms but only claimed HPMC, alternatives are dedicated to the public under Indivior v. Dr. Reddy's.

D. Vitiation / All-Elements Rule

Finding lipids equivalent to HPMC would vitiate the "release modifier" limitation - if ANY release mechanism qualifies, the limitation has no meaning.

E. Prior Art Limits Equivalents (Ensnarement)

Under Wilson Sporting Goods, a hypothetical claim covering lipid matrices would be invalid over prior art (30+ years of lipid matrix technology).

F. Public Dedication

Lipid matrix technology is well-established public domain - Compritol has been used commercially for 30+ years.

G. Insubstantial Differences Test Fails

The differences between hydrophilic polymer gels and hydrophobic lipid matrices are substantial, not insubstantial - different chemistry, physics, and biology.

Key Cases:
  • Festo Corp. v. Shoketsu Kinzoku, 535 U.S. 722 (2002) - Prosecution history estoppel
  • Wilson Sporting Goods v. David Geoffrey, 904 F.2d 677 (Fed. Cir. 1990) - Prior art limits equivalents
  • Pennwalt v. Durand-Wayland, 833 F.2d 931 (Fed. Cir. 1987) - "Way" requirement importance

6. Counter: "Unexpected Results Establish Non-Obviousness"

STRONG COUNTER

Veradermics argues: "We discovered an unexpected therapeutic window where hair growth occurs without cardiac side effects. The specific Cmax reduction with maintained efficacy was not predictable."

Our Response: Every Result Was Completely Predictable

A. ER Reduces Cmax - That's the DEFINITION of ER

Claiming "ER reduces Cmax" as unexpected is like claiming "water is wet." This is the fundamental purpose of extended-release technology.

B. The Therapeutic Window Was Already Clinically Known

Dermatologists were already exploiting the therapeutic window through off-label prescribing. Veradermics merely quantified what was functionally known.

C. Quantifying Known Effects Is Not Patentable

Under In re Aller: "It is not inventive to discover the optimum or workable ranges by routine experimentation."

D. Compare to TRUE Unexpected Results

In Allergan v. Sandoz (Lumigan), lower concentration was expected to have LESS efficacy but maintained it - results contradicted expectations.

Here, Veradermics' results perfectly match expectations:

Prediction Result Unexpected?
ER reduces Cmax Yes NO
Lower Cmax = fewer cardiac effects Yes NO
Oral minoxidil grows hair Yes NO

E. PK Parameters Are Inherent Properties

Under Persion v. Alvogen: "An obvious formulation cannot become nonobvious simply by administering it to a patient and claiming the resulting serum concentrations."

Key Cases:
  • In re Aller, 220 F.2d 454 (CCPA 1955) - Optimization of known ranges is obvious
  • Persion v. Alvogen, 945 F.3d 1184 (Fed. Cir. 2019) - Inherent PK cannot save obvious formulations
  • Allergan v. Sandoz, 796 F.3d 1293 (Fed. Cir. 2015) - TRUE unexpected results require contradiction

7. Counter: "KSR Obvious-to-Try Doesn't Apply"

STRONG COUNTER

Veradermics argues: "Under KSR, 'obvious to try' requires finite predictable solutions. ER minoxidil was NOT predictable because the therapeutic window was unknown, formulation variables were unpredictable, and success was not guaranteed."

Our Response: This IS the Quintessential "Obvious to Try" Case

A. Finite Number of ER Technologies

Under KSR: "When there are a finite number of identified, predictable solutions, a person of ordinary skill has good reason to pursue the known options."

There are approximately 5 major ER platforms:

  1. HPMC hydrophilic matrix
  2. Osmotic pump (OROS)
  3. Lipid/wax matrix
  4. Multi-particulate (coated pellets)
  5. Membrane-coated tablets

If Pfizer v. Apotex found 53 salt options "finite," then 5 ER platforms is certainly finite.

B. Success Was Reasonably Predictable

  • Oral minoxidil was already working for hair loss (off-label)
  • ER formulation predictably reduces Cmax
  • HPMC matrix behavior is well-characterized with mathematical models

C. Formulation Variables Are Routine Optimization

Under In re Aller, selecting HPMC grade (K4M vs K200M) and polymer percentage are classic "result-effective variables" - routine optimization, not invention.

D. Common Sense Applies Post-KSR

KSR: "A person of ordinary skill is also a person of ordinary creativity."

A POSA facing "how do we make oral minoxidil safer?" would logically think: reduce Cmax → use ER technology → select HPMC (industry standard). This is common sense, not innovation.

E. Clinical Proof Exceeds "Hope"

Veradermics had MORE than hope - they had clinical evidence from 1,404+ patients treated with off-label oral minoxidil. The "experiment" was merely confirmation of expected results.

Key Cases:
  • KSR v. Teleflex, 550 U.S. 398 (2007) - Finite predictable solutions = obvious to try
  • Pfizer v. Apotex, 480 F.3d 1348 (Fed. Cir. 2007) - 53 options was "finite"
  • In re Kubin, 561 F.3d 1351 (Fed. Cir. 2009) - Obvious to try applies even in complex fields
  • Alza v. Mylan, 464 F.3d 1286 (Fed. Cir. 2006) - ER formulation found obvious
